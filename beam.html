Theory

Aim / Objective

To determine the deflection of a simply supported beam when it is centrally loaded, and compare the experimental deflection with the theoretical value predicted by simple bending theory.

Whenever a beam is loaded, it deflects from its original position. The amount of deflection depends on the beam's cross-section and the bending moment acting on it.

In modern design, a beam must satisfy two criteria: strength (resisting bending moment and shear force) and stiffness (resisting excessive deflection).

Central deflection formula

yc = WL³ / 48EI

Here W is the central point load, L is the span between supports, E is the Young's Modulus of the beam material, and I is the second moment of area of the cross-section.

Viva Questions

Common viva-voce questions for this practical — tap a question to reveal the answer.

What is the objective of this experiment?
To determine the central deflection of a simply supported beam under a point load and compare it with the theoretical value predicted by simple bending theory.
What is meant by a simply supported beam?
A beam resting freely on two supports — one end typically a pin/hinge support and the other a roller support — which offer vertical reaction but no moment resistance.
Define deflection of a beam.
Deflection is the displacement of a point on a beam from its original unloaded position, measured perpendicular to the beam's longitudinal axis, caused by an applied load.
State the formula for central deflection and define each term.
yc = WL³ / 48EI, where W is the central point load, L is the span between supports, E is the Young's Modulus of the beam material, and I is the second moment of area (moment of inertia) of the cross-section.
What is Young's Modulus (E) and why does it matter here?
Young's Modulus is a material property describing stiffness — the ratio of stress to strain within the elastic limit. A higher E means the beam resists bending more, producing smaller deflection for the same load.
What is the moment of inertia (I), and how is it found for a rectangular section?
The moment of inertia measures a cross-section's resistance to bending. For a rectangular section it is I = bt³/12, where b is the width and t is the thickness (depth) of the beam.
Why does deflection increase with load?
Deflection is directly proportional to the applied load W (from y = WL³/48EI). As load increases, the bending moment along the beam increases proportionally, producing a proportionally larger deflection, as long as the beam stays within its elastic limit.
What assumptions are made in simple bending theory?
The beam material is homogeneous and isotropic, obeys Hooke's law, plane cross-sections remain plane after bending, the beam is initially straight, and deflections are small compared to the span.
What does a dial gauge measure, and how does it work?
A dial gauge measures small linear displacements. A spring-loaded plunger in contact with the beam moves as the beam deflects, and this linear motion is converted to a rotary needle movement via an internal rack-and-pinion mechanism, read off in divisions on the dial.
Why might the experimental and theoretical deflection values differ?
Differences arise from factors such as dial gauge parallax or calibration error, beam self-weight, imperfect support conditions, slight material inhomogeneity, and rounding in the measured span, load, or dimensions.
Where is beam deflection theory applied in real structures?
It's used to design and check floor beams, bridge girders, cranes, and machine shafts, ensuring they stay within allowable deflection limits so they remain safe, functional, and free of visible sagging or vibration issues.

• Reading must be taken in the unloading condition also.
